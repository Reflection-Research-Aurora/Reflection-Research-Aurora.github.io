Level 3

VIP Capstone

For highly motivated students seeking original work, personalized mentorship, and top-tier outcomes.

Program Timeline End Goals

Overview

A mentorship track for students ready to go further

VIP Capstone is a fully 1-on-1, assessment-based research track for students who already have strong technical foundations. This is not a class. It is a research collaboration. The program runs 16 weeks or more depending on the project scope.

This track is best for students aiming for unusually strong academic profiles, competitive scholarships, or standout research credentials that can support applications to selective STEM programs.

Service Details

What tailored mentorship looks like in Level 3

Research Gap Discovery

The student and mentor work together to identify a more original and defensible project direction.

Advanced Implementation

Support extends through data cleaning, model refinement, troubleshooting, and deeper research iteration.

Research Communication

Mentorship builds the depth of understanding needed to speak confidently about your work in any setting: interviews, presentations, or written materials. Students write their own application materials; we help them understand their project well enough to do so compellingly.

Timeline

A 16-week research progression

Phase 1 (Weeks 1–4)

Research setup: refining the research question, scanning relevant literature, identifying the right dataset, and agreeing on a methodology direction before implementation begins.

Phase 2 (Weeks 5–12)

Core implementation: data pipeline, model development, iterative refinement, and ablation. This is the longest phase. Results rarely arrive on the first attempt, and iteration is treated as part of the process, not a setback.

Phase 3 (Weeks 13–16+)

Output and submission: manuscript writing, presentation preparation, and targeting the appropriate venue: local showcase, national competition, or journal submission depending on readiness and project scope.

16 weeks is the baseline. Projects with broader scope or additional submission cycles run longer, and the timeline adjusts accordingly. There is no artificial deadline that compromises output quality.

End Goals

What this capstone track can aim toward

The end goal is professional-grade output rather than a generic student project. Outcomes are tiered by readiness and progress: local or regional student science showcases are the entry point, followed by national Canadian competitions such as the STEM Fellowship Big Data Challenge, and for students who are ready, submission to credible peer-reviewed journals, including the Briefings in Bioinformatics (IF ~9.5), Bioinformatics (IF ~5.8), BMC Bioinformatics, Computers (MDPI), PLOS ONE, or relevant Frontiers journals. Current cohort students have active submissions under revision at Oxford University Press journals in the top tier of the field.

Journal submission is a goal, not a paid guarantee. A well-executed, clearly scoped project is a stronger application asset than a rushed submission to a low-credibility venue. We only target journals where quality is the admission criterion.

VIP Capstone is where we can offer the most individualized and ambitious support for students who are ready to commit to a longer, more demanding process.

Upon completion, students receive a formal reference letter written by the tenure-track professor who supervised their research. This is not a letter confirming program enrollment. The professor writes from direct, extended observation of the student's work: the independent research question they identified, the original methodology they designed, the technical decisions they made and defended, and the results they produced over 16 or more weeks of 1-on-1 collaboration. Students at this level work on projects they helped define, on topics that have not been previously addressed, and the letter reflects that. Depending on how far the project progresses, the letter may also speak to a submitted or in-revision manuscript, a competition submission, or other concrete outputs the student produced. For scholarship panels and selective STEM programs, this is the kind of letter that is genuinely difficult to obtain elsewhere: a firsthand, credentialed account of what the student can do as a researcher, not as a course participant.
